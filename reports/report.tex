\begin{abstract}
This report summarizes Phase 05 of CostTrace: final network visualizations, statistical charts, and an IEEE-style evaluation of budget-constrained epidemic intervention on the SASHTS household contact network. The central question is which node-selection strategy maximizes infection reduction when only $k\%$ of nodes can be isolated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
COVID-19 contact tracing motivates a graph problem: after likely sources or high-risk contacts are identified, public health teams still need to decide whom to isolate under limited capacity. DeepTrace shows that graph neural networks and maximum-likelihood ideas can identify influential infection sources, but it does not directly optimize the downstream isolation decision. CostTrace fills this gap by formulating a budget-constrained top-$k$ intervention problem. The study compares Random, Degree, Betweenness, and GraphSAGE-based ranking, then evaluates each strategy through SIR simulation on real household proximity data.

\section{Data Source}
The dataset is the South Africa Household Transmission Study (SASHTS), containing 340 individuals and 542 weighted proximity edges across 88 household components. The processed graph removes invalid records and self-loops, preserves weighted contact duration, and keeps household-level connected components for intervention simulation. Average household size is 3.86, average component density is 0.963, and observed SARS-CoV-2 attack rate is 70.9\%.

\section{Methodology}
Node importance is measured with degree centrality, betweenness centrality, closeness centrality, weighted degree, and a composite risk score. Louvain community detection is applied with contact-duration weights; the result is 88 communities with modularity 0.970. The GNN component uses a weighted GraphSAGE classifier with topology, contact, demographic, and epidemiological features. The trained model reached validation AUC 0.804 and test AUC 0.767. Intervention is implemented as top-$k$ selection for budgets 1\%, 5\%, and 10\%, followed by SIR simulation with $\beta=0.25$, $\gamma=0.1$, $T=30$, and 50 Monte Carlo runs.

\section{Results and Evaluation}
The baseline simulation gives a mean of 3.42 infected nodes per household with standard deviation 1.64. The strongest result is the GNN strategy, which reduces mean infected nodes by 3.8\% at $k=1\%$, 22.2\% at $k=5\%$, and 42.7\% at $k=10\%$. At $k=1\%$, GNN exceeds Degree by 1.8 percentage points in SIR reduction.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
Phase 05 confirms that learned risk ranking is the most effective intervention rule in this repository's current experiments. The GNN strategy is especially valuable when budget is at least 5\%, while degree remains a competitive lightweight baseline. Limitations include the small household-level graph, a static rather than temporal graph representation, and an SIR model that does not yet filter recovered nodes before candidate selection. Future work should add recovery-aware top-$k$ filtering, dynamic temporal contacts, spatiotemporal features, and online intervention updates as new cases arrive.
